<h1 id="adenoids">Adenoids</h1><p>A reference article on adenoid hypertrophy and surgery.</p>
<pre><code>Overview: Snoring in infants is usually attributed to congenital laryngomalacia — a physiological issue. In older children, adenoid hypertrophy should be considered. The adenoids are lymphatic tissue in the nasopharynx; like the tonsils, they help the body fight bacteria and viruses. As children grow and their immune systems mature, the adenoids typically begin shrinking after age 6 and usually disappear around adolescence. The adenoids are located at the junction of the top and back walls of the nasopharynx — normally not visible when opening the mouth or looking through the nostrils. If the adenoids enlarge, they can block the sinuses and possibly the opening of the Eustachian tubes, causing sinusitis or middle ear infections. If a doctor suspects adenoid hypertrophy, in addition to examining the child's nose, ears, and throat, they will usually use a nasal endoscope (preferred) or X-ray (for uncooperative children) to make a more accurate assessment.

Some children are born with relatively large adenoids, but acute adenoid swelling often has an external trigger. Colds, allergic rhinitis, and sinusitis can all cause adenoid swelling. Controlling these external factors usually leads to significant improvement in most temporary cases. The first-line treatment for adenoid hypertrophy — and also the first-line treatment for allergic rhinitis (and sinusitis) — is a nasal corticosteroid spray (e.g., Nasonex). Leukotriene receptor antagonists such as montelukast sodium (brand name Singulair) are also commonly used. In addition to proper medication, nasal irrigation with saline is recommended for both allergic rhinitis and adenoid hypertrophy. Surgical indications for adenoidectomy include: (1) breathing difficulties, including frequent snoring, sleep apnea, and labored breathing; (2) severe obstructive sleep apnea caused by adenoid hypertrophy; (3) frequent colds with rhinitis or sinusitis that is difficult to resolve; (4) recurrent middle ear infections, middle ear effusion, or the need for two or more sets of ear tubes. If parents are uncertain, a sleep study can be arranged.</code></pre>
