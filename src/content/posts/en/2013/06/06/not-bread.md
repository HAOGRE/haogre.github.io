I stumbled across Murakami's "The Second Bakery Attack" again.

In the dead of night, a newlywed couple woke up ravenously hungry, swept every scrap of food from the kitchen — and still the hunger raged, fierce and inexplicable.

This was no ordinary hunger. "I've never been this hungry in my life," the wife said. And then, almost involuntarily, the narrator let slip: "I once robbed a bakery." *What does that even mean?* she pressed. He explained: years ago, he and his best friend had gone to rob a bakery — not for money, just for bread.

The heist went smoothly; the owner didn't resist. But as a trade, he wanted the two young men to sit with him and listen to Wagner. They hesitated, then agreed. After all, that made it an exchange, not a robbery. They walked out with bread.

Yet for days afterward, the two of them wrestled with the question: was the robbery the right call, or was the exchange better? Rationally, the exchange was fine — it was legal, at least. But instinctively, the narrator felt something had gone wrong, something important and murky. They should have used the knife, grabbed the bread, and left. That feeling was mutual. Not long after, the two friends drifted apart for no clear reason.

Telling his wife the story, the narrator said: "We always felt there was a serious mistake in what we did — some dark shadow it left on our lives. There's no question we were cursed." "Not just you," his wife replied. "I feel cursed too." She believed this was the source of their strange, violent hunger.

To break the curse, they would have to finish what was never finished — rob a bakery for real. And so the newlyweds drove out in the middle of the night, masks and a shotgun ready, and robbed a bakery — a McDonald's.

Search "The Second Bakery Attack" and you'll find the unfinished curse strikes a chord with a lot of people.

It's graduation season again, and I still owe myself a graduation trip — spectacular or ordinary, I don't care — it's a debt to myself that needs paying. Another unfinished bakery.

---

Hours slip away after work without a trace, and in the end they add up to a featureless, forgettable blur.

I clock out at five. What do I do with that?

- **17:00–18:00** 　Dinner and getting settled, at the very least
- **18:00–18:30** 　A review of the day's work, probably enough time for that
- **18:30–23:30** 　What have I actually been doing with this time?

A while back, during a trip to Beijing, I overheard someone mention a grad-school senior who found her job so monotonous — nine-to-five, day in day out — that she eventually enrolled in a master's program at a dental hospital. Nobody quite knows how that happened.

In my current state, a plot twist like that seems unlikely. Everyone's different. What matters is what I do with those hours after five.

The short version: survive by day, grow by night. If I actually lived by that, I'd become a different person entirely.

No room for what-ifs.

I don't want to stay the person I am now. What will the future me look like? If I keep walking this road all the way to the top — will I be happy? "Happy" is too big a word. Will I just be okay?

Heaven knows.

A clock that can't get up in the morning.

---

Hard work — that's advice for kids. Nobody's going to pull me aside and tell me how to grind my way to greatness anymore.

Like the hands of a clock: a whole lifetime, never forming any real angle with the face it runs on. Is that what effort looks like?

Unfinished.

So many stories, so many regrets — they pass. Accept them or not, they pass.

I can feel myself growing up, little by little, working through the unfinished things before they have a chance to become regrets — making sure they get finished.
