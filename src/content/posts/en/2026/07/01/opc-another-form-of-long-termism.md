Pieter Levels built an AI photo app with a single PHP file, now making $100k/month. No team, no VC, no HR, no OKR meetings.

This got me thinking: Is the one-person company (OPC) a fringe path or a seriously undervalued choice?

Here's what I think so far.

---

## 1. OPC Is Not What Most People Think

Many hear "one-person company" and assume it's just a fancy term for freelancer. It's not.

Freelancing trades time for money—stop working, stop earning. VC startups trade capital for speed—investors want 10x or an exit. OPC is different: it uses **leverage** to amplify time. You sleep, code runs, users pay.

The core logic: **Replace labor with systems, not with employees.**

---

## 2. Why Now? Three Levers Have Matured

OPC isn't new, but it's only recently become viable.

### 1. Code Lever

Once software is written, serving 1 user vs. 1 million users has nearly the same marginal cost. Pieter Levels puts it bluntly: "100% automation, 99% profit margins."

Can't code? There's a second lever.

### 2. AI Lever

Post-2024, AI tools aren't just nice-to-haves—they're replacing entire teams. On Indie Hackers, someone said adding an AI workflow cut LinkedIn management from 10 hours/week to 10 minutes. Levels himself barely hand-writes code anymore, relying on Claude Code on a VPS.

AI replaces execution, not judgment. One person today can output what a small team could five years ago.

### 3. Distribution Lever

The internet removed geographic barriers. A developer in Chengdu can sell to users in San Francisco. No previous era offered this to one-person companies.

---

## 3. Where High Profit Comes From

Traditional profits get eaten by labor, rent, inventory, and management. OPC eliminates almost all of them.

- **Labor cost:** just yourself and a few AI subscriptions
- **Rent:** a server and a café Wi-Fi
- **Inventory:** digital products, zero inventory
- **Management:** no team, no management

Levels achieves nearly 99% margins simply because his business model has no high-cost items.

On pricing, subscriptions beat one-time fees. One-time fees require finding new customers every month; subscriptions are compound—renewals accumulate naturally, growing MRR even without marketing.

---

## 4. Real Pitfalls

I don't want to make this sound easy. Indie Hackers has cautionary tales.

**Income volatility is real.** One person's monthly revenue dropped from $2,800 to $340 in two months. Product-market fit shifts, algorithms change, competitors emerge—OPCs have weaker buffers.

**All roles fall on one person.** Product, design, development, marketing, support—while coding, no one does growth; while handling complaints, no one builds features. Fragmentation is structural.

**Growth ceilings exist.** Paul Jarvis says staying small by choice isn't failure—true. But if your goal is a billion-dollar company, OPC isn't the path.

Recognizing these helps you decide if it's for you, not to scare you off.

---

## 5. My Take

**OPC isn't escaping employment.** Many talk about it with an undertone of "not wanting a boss." But the real challenge isn't the absence of a superior—it's playing customer, PM, sales, and engineer simultaneously, bearing all consequences. It's not easier than a job; it's a different structure.

**The core skill is turning your own problem into a product.** Levels built Nomad List because he himself was a digital nomad who needed it. Starting from "what do I need?" gives more direct information and lower validation cost than researching "what does the market need?"

**OPC suits people with a leverage mindset.** If your first instinct is always "I'll just work more hours," you'll fall into self-exploitation. This path demands constantly asking: Can this be handed to a tool or system?

I'm seriously considering OPC because I've come to see it as a form of long-termism. One person handling product, engineering, and operations sounds heavy, but you're not running a company—you're running your life. Experiencing every role, owning every decision—the density of growth may be unmatched by a decade in a corporate role.

---

OPC isn't a lifestyle aesthetic or an anti-system stance. It's an economic structure: using leverage so one person's creativity generates value beyond their time limit.

Whether it works depends on whether you have something to leverage—skills, knowledge, insight into a problem, or a unique perspective.

If you do, it's worth a serious thought.
