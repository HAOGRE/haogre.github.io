A few days ago, I scolded my son.

The situation wasn't complicated. Recently, I noticed his attitude slipping on a few tasks—he seemed listless, doing the bare minimum, just getting by. I watched, growing more anxious, and my criticism escalated. I wanted him to be more serious and proactive, not just coasting.

At the time, I thought my words were justified. As a parent, you can't ignore when your child is slacking off. Some habits, if not corrected now, might become harder to change later.

A few days later, he pulled out a textbook and showed me a story titled "'Wonderful' and 'Terrible'."

![Son showing the lesson "Wonderful" and "Terrible"](/uploads/2026/07/10/他没有反驳我，只是递来一篇课文/01-课本照片.jpg)

In the story, a child writes his first poem. His mother says it's "wonderful"; his father says it's "terrible." Both are talking about the same poem, yet their evaluations are completely opposite. The child later realizes that both his mother's encouragement and his father's criticism come from love and both shape him.

When my son handed me that page, I understood what he was trying to say.

He wasn't telling me to only praise him from now on, or that he should never be criticized. He just wanted me to know that a father's words carry more weight than the father himself realizes.

I saw a few tasks not done well and thought I was reminding him to improve. But to a child, "you didn't do this well" and "you have a bad attitude" might sound completely different. The former leaves room for improvement; the latter feels like a verdict. Said more harshly, he might hear not "Dad wants me to do better" but "Dad thinks I'm a slacker."

I used to think that clear, direct criticism would make children take things seriously. Now I realize that harsh words only prove the father is anxious, not necessarily that the child knows what to do next. Overwhelmed by emotion, he might just feel sad, defensive, or eager to end the conversation. The message I actually wanted to convey might not get through at all.

This doesn't mean those tasks are off the hook. They still need to be done, and slacking still needs to be addressed. But next time, I'll change the order.

I can start by asking him what happened, why he didn't want to do it, or where he got stuck—let him finish his thoughts. Then I'll only talk about what I observed: what wasn't completed, what was just going through the motions, and what impact it had on others. Finally, I'll be specific about expectations: redo it, or agree on a standard for next time. When criticism is about the task, the child knows what to fix. Giving him a chance to try again turns the demand into something more than just a father's angry words.

![Listen first, then talk about how to do better](/uploads/2026/07/10/他没有反驳我，只是递来一篇课文/02-先听他说完.png)

Encouragement doesn't mean saying everything is fine. Children need to know they are capable of doing things well, and they also need to know where they fall short. But they should hear both. If there's only praise, they may not know boundaries; if there's only criticism, they may lose the energy to try again.

I don't know how long my son thought before pulling out that story. Maybe he just happened to be studying it and saw the parallel to our situation. For me, it was a clear response. He showed me the story, and also showed me what a child hears when standing on the other side.

That story didn't excuse him from his responsibilities. It reminded me that being a father isn't just about judging. Next time, before I speak, I should listen to him first, and then work together to do what needs to be done.
