Today I took my child for the semi-annual routine eye exam. The most critical line on the report was axial length: left eye grew 0.04 mm compared to six months ago, right eye 0.03 mm.

A sheet of A4 paper is about 0.1 mm thick. In half a year, my child's eyeball lengthened by less than half a sheet.

This number looks like nothing. But it's the only receipt the instrument can read for all the arrangements over the past six months—pushing him outdoors every day, controlling near-work duration, showing up for checkups on time.

> This article is a parent's record and compilation of information, not medical advice. Please follow your doctor's instructions for specific interventions.

## 1. Why axial length matters more than prescription on the report

Parents instinctively focus on prescription because it's a language they understand. But prescription is an unreliable metric.

It's affected by the day's accommodation state, the refraction method, and whether the child is tired. A difference of 50 to 100 degrees before and after cycloplegia is common. The same child tested in the morning and afternoon may get different results.

Axial length doesn't. It's a physical length measured by optical biometry, with single-measurement repeatability on the order of 0.01–0.02 mm. It doesn't care how the child feels today.

More importantly, two things:

**First, axial length is principal, prescription is interest.** Every 1 mm of axial length growth corresponds to roughly 250–300 degrees of myopia. Changes in prescription are the projection of eyeball elongation onto the retina.

**Second, axial length is irreversible.** The cornea can be reshaped, prescription can be corrected, vision can be improved—but once the eyeball lengthens, it never goes back. Any claim that "prescription has come down" will eventually be reversed on paper if axial length hasn't stopped.

So from the day the file is created, the only column that needs continuous recording and comparison is axial length. That's why the National Health Commission's "Myopia Prevention and Control Guidelines (2024 Edition)" mandates establishing refractive development files starting from kindergarten, with axial length as the core field.

## 2. What 0.04 mm means—first, establish a reference

This is the most easily misunderstood part: **Normal children's eyeballs also grow.**

The goal of control is never "zero axial length growth." Zero growth means the eyeball stops developing, which is not good. The real goal is to shave off the "myopic" layer of axial growth, leaving only the "physiological" layer.

![Two layers of growth: physiological and myopic](/uploads/2026/07/19/另一种长期主义：儿童的近视防控/02-two-layers-of-growth.png)

<small>Figure 02 | Two layers of growth: physiological and myopic</small>

Physiological axial growth rates in emmetropic (non-myopic) children are consistent across several independent cohorts:

| Age | Annual growth in emmetropes | Source |
|---|---|---|
| 6–9 years | 0.14–0.20 mm/year | European, Chinese cohorts |
| 8 years | 0.14–0.15 mm/year | OLSM / SCORM models |
| 10 years | 0.09 mm/year | OLSM / SCORM models |
| 12 years | 0.06 mm/year | OLSM / SCORM models |
| After 12 | ~0.10 mm/year, declining | Guangzhou Twin Eye Study (GTES) |

In contrast, **myopic children without intervention**: typically ≥0.3 mm/year under 10, and about 0.2 mm/year around puberty. That is, myopic children's eyeballs grow one to two times faster than their emmetropic peers.

Common clinical benchmarks for judging control:

- Under 10 years: annual growth <0.3 mm is controlled, <0.1–0.2 mm is excellent (close to emmetropic peers)
- Over 10 years: <0.2 mm is ideal, <0.1 mm is excellent

Back to our report. Half-year growth of 0.04 and 0.03 mm annualizes to 0.08 and 0.06 mm. Whether the child is 7 or 14, these numbers fall at or below the lower edge of the natural growth range for emmetropic peers.

In plain English: **Over the past six months, his eyes were basically just "growing with his body," not "becoming myopic."**

By the way, the 0.01 mm difference between eyes is noise—don't worry about it. The instrument's single-measurement repeatability is at that level. What truly warrants attention: one eye consistently growing faster than the other over multiple visits, or the inter-eye difference widening to over 0.3 mm.

## 3. Why 0.04 mm is worth fighting for—the endgame is 40 years later

If the goal were just "don't exceed 600 degrees by college entrance exams," this level of millimeter-scale management would be overkill.

But the real objective function isn't prescription—it's **his retina at age 60**.

A study of over 10,000 Dutch people (average age 61) showed this curve: those with axial length <26 mm had a 3.8% lifetime risk of myopia-related visual impairment; those with axial length ≥26 mm saw the risk jump to 25%. Further, the 26–28 mm group had twice the risk at age 60, 28–30 mm had 11 times, and over 30 mm had 25 times—and 90% of those with axial length over 30 mm developed visual impairment by age 75.

26 mm is a steep dividing line. The real cost of high myopia isn't thick glasses—it's irreversible conditions like retinal detachment, macular degeneration, and glaucoma, which start to concentrate in the 40s and 50s.

![The 26 mm fork: two slopes of eight-year compounding](/uploads/2026/07/19/另一种长期主义：儿童的近视防控/03-the-26mm-fork.png)

<small>Figure 03 | The 26 mm fork: two slopes of eight-year compounding</small>

Let's make it arithmetic. Suppose a 10-year-old with axial length 24.5 mm, with 8 years until growth stops around age 18:

- Growing 0.3 mm/year → +2.4 mm over 8 years → **26.9 mm**, into high-risk territory
- Growing 0.1 mm/year → +0.8 mm over 8 years → **25.3 mm**, staying safe

A difference of 0.2 mm per year is invisible to the naked eye, but after eight years, it's two completely different life trajectories.

That's the precise meaning of long-termism in this context: **the benefit of a single decision is too small to see that day, but it compounds annually, and the payoff comes decades later.**

## 4. What's in the toolbox, and how many mm each is worth

The International Myopia Institute (IMI) 2025 intervention review summarized the efficacy of major tools, with units standardized to "mm less axial growth compared to control":

| Tool | 1 year | 2 years | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0.01% atropine | 0.08 mm | 0.12 mm | Second-year efficacy about half of first; low concentration, little rebound |
| 0.05% atropine | — | 3-year efficacy >2x that of 0.01% | LAMP study; higher concentration and younger age at cessation → more rebound |
| Orthokeratology (OK lenses) | 0.17 mm | 0.30 mm | Strongest single tool; efficacy correlates with baseline myopia |
| Defocus spectacle lenses (DIMS / HAL) | ~0.17 mm | 0.34–0.35 mm | Efficacy sustained beyond first year |
| Soft multifocal contact lenses | 0.03–0.19 mm | 0.06–0.23 mm | Higher add power designs more effective; 3-year 0.07–0.28 mm |
| OK lenses + 0.01% atropine | 0.12 mm more than OK alone | — | Atropine add-on effect decays yearly (year 1: 0.09, year 2: 0.02) |

![The toolbox is all speed bumps, no brakes](/uploads/2026/07/19/另一种长期主义：儿童的近视防控/04-all-brakes-no-stop.png)

<small>Figure 04 | The toolbox is all speed bumps, no brakes</small>

Three takeaways from this table.

**First, none is a "cure."** The strongest single tool buys about 0.3 mm over two years. An untreated young myopic child might grow 0.6 mm in the same period. Tools can only flatten the slope; time keeps passing.

**Second, starting early matters more than choosing the strongest tool.** A regimen started at age 8 that saves 0.15 mm per year accumulates far more than the strongest regimen started at age 12—because the fastest-growing years are already gone, and those millimeters can't be recovered.

**Third, don't switch between regimens frequently.** All efficacy numbers in the table are measured under "continuous use." Each switch incurs an adaptation period and data discontinuity. The ATLAS study of atropine followed patients for 18 years and found that after cessation, progression rates converged—what ultimately remains is the millimeters saved during those early years.

## 5. The only primary prevention: outdoor time

All the tools above work only for children who are already myopic. They are brakes, not vaccines.

Outdoor activity is one of the few interventions proven by randomized controlled trials to **delay the onset of myopia**:

- A 3-year cluster-randomized trial in Guangzhou: adding 40 minutes of outdoor activity during school hours reduced cumulative myopia incidence from 39.5% to 30.4%, a relative reduction of nearly one-quarter.
- A trial in Taiwan: 80 minutes of outdoor time daily reduced incidence by about half.
- Multiple meta-analyses support a cumulative 2 hours per day as an effective threshold.

![Effective outdoor time depends on illuminance, not green](/uploads/2026/07/19/另一种长期主义：儿童的近视防控/05-lux-not-green.png)

<small>Figure 05 | Effective outdoor time depends on illuminance, not green</small>

But "outdoor" is easily misinterpreted. The key variable is **light intensity**, not greenery or physical activity:

- Ambient illuminance needs to reach at least 1000 lux to start being meaningful; 2000–3000 lux is better
- Even on cloudy days, outdoor illuminance is several thousand lux; classrooms typically have only a few hundred lux
- So hanging out in a hallway doesn't count, nor does being behind glass—glass cuts a significant portion of effective illuminance
- No need to accumulate two hours in one go; multiple sessions of 15–20 minutes or more are equally effective

This is the cheapest intervention, yet the hardest to implement because it directly competes with homework time. Precisely because it competes for time, it needs to be scheduled into the daily routine, not left to "whenever there's time."

## 6. Make it a process, not willpower

Long-termism can't survive ten years on willpower alone. It has to become a process that doesn't require re-deciding every time.

**1. Fixed rhythm, fixed equipment, fixed person.** Every six months, same hospital, same biometer. Systematic errors between different devices can eat up half a year's real growth, leading you to misjudge the situation.

**2. Look only at slope, not single points.** Record each axial length measurement in the same table, calculate the annualized rate, and compare it to age-matched physiological values. Any single data point may have noise; two consecutive points in the same direction constitute a signal.

**3. Don't change regimens for one fluctuation.** The threshold for switching should be "two consecutive annualized rates exceeding twice the age-matched physiological value," not "this time was 0.02 mm more than last time." Frequent adjustments cause data discontinuity that hurts more than the difference between regimens.

**4. Increase monitoring during holidays, don't relax.** Winter and summer breaks are high-risk windows for myopia progression—more indoor time, more screens, disrupted routines. Don't let go during these periods.

## 7. A few counterintuitive points

**Prescription not increasing doesn't mean it's controlled.** Prescription is affected by accommodation; axial length is the real ledger. Sometimes refraction is unchanged while axial length has grown 0.2 mm—that's real deterioration, just not yet reflected on the prescription.

**Axial length increasing isn't necessarily bad.** When height grows, axial length also grows, and corneal curvature compensates partially. So what matters is the "net excess relative to age-matched physiological values," not the absolute number.

**The endpoint of control is not "no myopia," but "no high myopia."** In today's visual environment, setting the goal as the former will break the parents first, then the child, and the whole effort will be abandoned. Setting the goal as "axial length ≤26 mm at age 18" is a goal that can sustain ten years of effort.

## 8. Conclusion

On today's report, 0.04 and 0.03 are not a score—they are a confirmation that the seemingly unremarkable arrangements of the past six months have indeed worked.

The term "long-termism" has been overused in investing. Its meaning here is much simpler: everything you do has an effect too small to see that day; you need to keep doing it for ten years; and the final check comes forty years later, in a single fundus photo.

Half a year, 0.04 mm. Next half year, same plan.
