“One monk carries water to drink, two monks carry water together, and three monks have no water to drink.”

This proverb is usually used to explain what happens when too many people fail to act as one. It is also often reduced to a story about selfishness, laziness, or a lack of responsibility. Looked at from another angle, it resembles a simplified model of cooperation: everyone shares the benefit of having water, but the cost of fetching it must be borne by whoever does the work. When the rules fail to connect those benefits and costs, cooperation can come to a standstill.

This is not a discussion of the monks’ character. It is a discussion of incentives and rules.

## 1. “Why should I be the one to go?”

The most direct explanation is the free-rider problem. In a shared water-supply arrangement, everyone gets water, but fetching it takes time and effort. If someone else has already gone, you can enjoy the benefit without paying the cost. If everyone thinks this way, no one fetches the water.

There is an easily overlooked detail here: water itself is not strictly a public good. Once one person drinks a bowl of water, others cannot drink that same bowl, and the temple can also restrict who is allowed to collect it. More precisely, **drinking the water is a shared benefit, while the labor of supplying it must be carried out by an individual**. The free-rider problem appears in the “joint production” stage; the final product does not have to be a textbook public good.

With only one monk, the benefits and costs of fetching water fall on the same person, so there is no coordination problem. Once three people live together, each can wait for someone else to act. The least-effort choice for each individual then conflicts with what the group needs.

## 2. This is not quite the “tragedy of the commons”

The original story is sometimes classified directly as a tragedy of the commons, but the two situations are different.

The tragedy of the commons usually concerns a rival shared resource, such as a pasture, fishery, or groundwater basin. Everyone can use the resource, but when one person takes a little more, part of the cost is spread across the group. Eventually, overuse depletes the resource. In the story of the three monks, the problem is “who will provide the water,” not “everyone has drained a shared well.”

If the temple’s well is genuinely limited and the monks’ use of it affects one another, then the story also involves a common-resource governance problem. Otherwise, free-riding or a collective-action problem is the more accurate description. Research on governing common resources also shows that privatization and top-down control are not the only options. Clear boundaries, monitoring, and enforceable rules can support long-term cooperation as well.[1]

## 3. It is closer to a “volunteer’s dilemma” than a prisoner’s dilemma

We can represent the monks’ choices with a simple model. As long as one person fetches water each day, everyone can drink. The person who fetches it bears the labor cost, while the others enjoy the result.

For any given monk, the best strategy depends on what the others do:

- If someone else has already fetched the water, staying behind is the best deal: you can simply drink;
- If no one has fetched it, going yourself at least ensures that everyone has water;
- If you fetch the water and someone else does too, everyone has water, but you have taken on extra labor that could have been avoided.

This is not a simple game in which “do not fetch water” is always the dominant strategy. It is closer to a multiplayer volunteer’s dilemma: everyone wants someone to bear the cost, but no one wants to be that person. Without a rotation, compensation, or a clear commitment, the three monks may keep waiting for someone else to move first.

So “three monks have no water” is not an outcome that rationality makes inevitable. It is an outcome that becomes likely when rules are missing. In real life, trust, reputation, repeated interaction, and ad hoc division of labor can all move the result back toward cooperation.

## 4. As the group grows, coordination is no longer free

When two people carry water together, the task already contains a division of labor: they walk together and carry together, making it harder for either person to pretend they did not participate. With three people, new questions appear: Who goes? Should one person carry the water, or should two people carry it together? Who is responsible today, and how will the schedule rotate tomorrow? What happens if someone gets sick or is temporarily unavailable?

The communication, monitoring, and confirmation required to establish and enforce cooperation are all forms of transaction cost. As the number of participants grows, potential coordination relationships usually become more complicated, but they do not necessarily grow at a geometric rate. What often causes cooperation to fail is not the number of people itself, but the team’s failure to address these questions in advance through explicit rules.

## 5. What rules should the abbot design?

Mechanism design starts by acknowledging that participants will make choices according to their own interests, then adjusts the rules so that individual choices are more aligned with collective needs. In essence, mechanism design studies how institutions can be designed so that individual actions, under particular information and incentive conditions, produce the intended collective outcome.[2]

Applied to the temple, several simple arrangements are possible.

**A rotating duty schedule** assigns water-fetching to specific days and establishes how people can swap shifts. This saves everyone from renegotiating the task every day and prevents responsibility gaps such as “I thought he was going.”

**Compensation** gives the person who fetches water extra water rations, more rest time, or one fewer household chore. Compensation does not have to be monetary. What matters is that the person bearing the cost receives a predictable return.

**A division of labor** groups tasks such as fetching water, chopping wood, and cooking, then rotates those assignments regularly. The point is not to lock anyone permanently into one role, but to make sure everyone knows what they are responsible for—and what everyone else is responsible for.

**Monitoring and fallback arrangements** add a simple record-keeping and replacement process. For example, if the person on duty fails to complete the task, the next person covers for them, and the schedule is reconciled at the end of the month. A duty roster without an enforcement mechanism is only a wish list.

These arrangements do not all need to be used at once. The number of participants, task frequency, and ease of monitoring all affect which arrangement will work best. Rules that operate reliably tend to share three features: responsibilities are clear, benefits and costs are broadly aligned, and exceptions have a defined procedure.

## Conclusion

“Three monks have no water to drink” is, first of all, a story about a failed water-supply mechanism. When someone on a team is unwilling to take on a shared task, it helps to check three things first: Who is responsible? How are they compensated? What happens if no one completes the task?

Reducing the problem to human nature cannot explain why the same people make different choices under different rules. Change the rules, and the outcome of cooperation may change too.
