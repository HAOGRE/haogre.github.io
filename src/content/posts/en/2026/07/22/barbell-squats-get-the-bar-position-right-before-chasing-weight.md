Yesterday was my first time going to the gym.

I used several machines and did three exercises, including barbell squats and a back exercise. Overall, it felt better than I expected: my body was definitely engaged, but it was not nearly as intimidating as I had imagined. More importantly, I began to realize something: fitness and health are not the same thing.

Health is more like the body’s current condition. Fitness is the active practice of maintaining and improving it. When we are young, it is easy to assume that the body will remain in decent shape on its own. But at my age, simply “not being sick” no longer feels like enough. Strength, mobility, and physical stability also deserve a deliberate place in everyday life.

The barbell squat left the strongest impression on me during this workout. It looks like nothing more than “putting a barbell on your back, squatting down, and standing up,” but it actually requires the feet, knees, hips, torso, shoulders, and upper back to work together. For someone just starting out, weight is not the first problem to solve. Bar placement and movement path are.

## The short version: beginners should start with the high bar

There are two common ways to position a barbell for a back squat: high bar and low bar.

In a high-bar squat, the bar rests on the upper trapezius—the muscular “shelf” above the shoulders, close to the base of the neck but not directly on the bones of the cervical spine. In a low-bar squat, the bar sits lower, usually on the muscles around the rear deltoids and shoulder blades.

If you are just starting out, I would suggest beginning with the high bar. That does not mean the high bar is inherently “more correct”; it is simply usually easier to understand. The torso stays relatively upright, the movement feels more like a natural squat, and the demands on the hips, shoulders, and upper back are more intuitive. The low bar can shift more of the work toward the hips, glutes, and legs, but it requires greater shoulder mobility, upper-back tension, and movement control. It is better explored after you are familiar with the squat.

## The key to bar placement: rest it on muscle, not on your neck

Before getting under the bar, turn your upper back into a stable shelf. Grip the bar with both hands and pull your shoulder blades back and down to create tension through the upper back. If necessary, narrow your grip slightly until your back feels like it has formed a solid platform.

When you move under the bar, it should make contact with the muscles of your upper back. You can find the high-bar position roughly as follows:

1. Stand in front of the rack and begin with an empty bar or very light weight.
2. Tighten your shoulder blades and upper back so the upper trapezius rises.
3. Let the bar settle onto the plane formed by those muscles.
4. Raise your head slightly to confirm that the bar is not pressing directly on your cervical spine, then return your head to a natural position.
5. Grip the bar symmetrically and avoid forcing your wrists into an extreme angle.

A practical test is this: the bar should not cause obvious sharp pain or numbness in your neck, and you should not need to “hold it up” with your arms. It should sit securely on your upper back. Your hands are mainly there to control the bar, not to bear its entire weight.

The low-bar position is lower, with the bar resting on the muscles around the rear deltoids and shoulder blades. It is not a matter of placing the bar as low on your back as possible, and it should never rest on the hard bones of the shoulder blades. If finding the low-bar position requires forcing your wrists into a bend, pinching your shoulders, or causing shoulder pain, that variation may not be suitable for you yet.

## How to perform a complete squat

### 1. Set up the squat rack

Adjust the hooks to a suitable height. When standing under the bar, you should not need to stand on tiptoe or raise your heels significantly to unrack it. After taking the bar out, step back only one or two small steps and settle into a safe position. Avoid walking around while carrying the bar.

If the rack has safety pins or safety bars, set them just below the lowest point of your squat. That way, if a repetition fails, you can place the bar on the safeties instead of forcing yourself to finish the lift.

### 2. Establish a stable base

Your feet can be slightly wider than hip-width, with your toes turned out naturally. There is no single stance that works for everyone. Leg length, hip structure, and ankle mobility all affect the right choice.

Keep the foot grounded at three points: the base of the big toe, the base of the little toe, and the heel. Do not deliberately shift your weight onto your heels just to keep your knees from “ever going past your toes.” Some forward knee travel is normal. What matters is keeping the feet stable and the knees generally aligned with the toes.

### 3. Breathe and brace your torso

Before descending, inhale into your abdomen and torso, then brace as if preparing to absorb a light impact. This does not mean sucking your stomach flat. It means creating pressure around your midsection to help stabilize your torso.

When you are starting out, there is no need to pursue complicated breathing techniques. Use a weight you can control and do not hold your breath until you feel dizzy. If you experience dizziness, chest tightness, or any other discomfort, stop immediately and rest.

### 4. Descend: let the knees and hips move together

Begin the descent by moving your hips back and down while bending your knees in the direction of your toes. Imagine sitting down between your feet rather than simply pushing your hips backward.

During the descent, the bar should travel along a path that is close to vertical and remain roughly above the middle of your foot. Your torso can lean forward naturally, but do not let your lower back collapse or round. At the same time, do not overextend your back just to keep your torso “upright.”

Use the deepest position you can control with stability. You can often descend until your thighs are roughly parallel to the floor or lower. But if going deeper causes your heels to lift, your knees to collapse inward, your back to lose its neutral position, or your hips to tuck noticeably, stop at a higher position for now and gradually improve your mobility and control.

### 5. Stand up while maintaining the bar path

As you rise from the bottom, imagine pushing the floor apart with your feet. Extend your knees and hips together, keeping your chest and hips rising as much as possible at the same rate. Avoid letting your hips shoot up first while your upper body struggles to catch up.

Once you reach standing, you do not need to forcefully lock your knees or lean backward. A stable, repeatable repetition is already a successful repetition.

## Common mistakes beginners make

First, placing the bar directly on the neck. This puts unnecessary pressure on the cervical area and often indicates that the upper back has not yet formed a stable shelf.

Second, adding weight too quickly. If your movement is not stable but you keep adding plates, you may end up practicing “moving the weight a little through sheer effort” rather than practicing the squat itself. An empty bar, bodyweight squats, and box squats can all help you develop the right feel.

Third, letting the knees collapse inward during the descent. The knees do not need to be forced dramatically outward, but they should generally track in the same direction as the toes. Reduce the weight and shorten the range of motion if necessary, then rebuild control through the feet and hips.

Fourth, chasing a fixed depth. Everyone has different proportions. “You must squat to a certain depth” is not more important than being stable, pain-free, and in control.

Fifth, walking too far when unracking or reracking the bar. Set the rack, safety pins, and stepping path before you begin. After the final repetition, make sure the bar has made contact with the hooks before relaxing your body.

## My plan for the first stage

For now, I am not setting a target for how much weight I should squat. My first goal is to make the movement stable:

- Begin each session with bodyweight squats or an empty-bar warm-up, checking that the ankles, knees, hips, and lower back feel normal.
- Use a weight I can control through the full movement, leaving a few repetitions in reserve rather than allowing my form to break down.
- Watch from the side to see whether the bar is moving roughly above the middle of the foot.
- Change only one variable at a time—for example, stance width, toe angle, or bar position—instead of changing everything at once.
- If I experience persistent joint pain, sharp pain, numbness, chest tightness, or dizziness, I will stop training and, if necessary, ask a coach or medical professional for an assessment.

For a first visit to the gym, simply coming away with a sense of satisfaction is already a good thing. But the more meaningful beginning is not lifting a particular amount of weight. It is gradually learning to understand your body: what is producing force, what needs stability, and when you should stop.

The barbell squat was only one of the three exercises I did, but it was enough to remind me that fitness is not about fighting your body. It is about rebuilding a relationship with it. Put the bar in the right place first; then worry about weight, repetitions, and progress. That order may matter more than any training plan.
