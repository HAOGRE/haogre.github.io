The boy who was once dragged out of internet cafes by his ears has grown up — and become the furious parent catching his own child playing mobile games past midnight.

Gaming is human nature. But children are not yet rational adults. Before they have developed good judgment — one of life's most important skills — appropriate intervention and guidance are necessary. What counts as appropriate? The prescription is in the parents' hands.

---

One line from a primary school student in a news interview has stayed with me: "When I'm lonely, my mom gives me her phone."

It's hard not to reflect: when middle and primary school students fall into gaming addiction, a large part of the problem is a failure of family education.

A person's world is defined by what they can reach. The moment a new life enters the world, their understanding of it begins at zero and grows through addition. A child's earliest world is their parents' living environment. Take a child on a trip somewhere far, and a sense of the distant world takes root in their heart — one day they'll know that beyond this small planet there is a solar system, and beyond that a boundless universe. Give a child a book, and in its finite pages they'll meet countless friends: they'll learn that Jing Ke was no woman but a towering man standing at the Yishui River, that Li Bai's closest companions were poetry and wine and not the knife-edge assassin he's sometimes romanticized as.

Good parents, then, are lamp-bearers — illuminating the path ahead. What they do and think determines the scope of their child's world. If a child discovers more interesting things in a wider world, will mobile games still hold such exclusive power over them?

Children love games by nature. But if engaging, healthy outdoor activities can replace virtual ones, you not only satisfy their drive for challenge and achievement — you also build their capacity for connection with others and with society. Isn't that worth something?

A friend of mine takes his daughter to a children's theater production one day, makes crafts with her the next, or — without going anywhere at all — the two of them sprawl on the sofa and build a story together, line by line, spending a perfectly happy afternoon. A family life this rich and varied: that child is unlikely to lose herself in a game and never find her way back.
